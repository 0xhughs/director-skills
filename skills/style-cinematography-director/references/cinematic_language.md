# Cinematic Language

Use cinematic terms as operational controls, not decoration.

## Shot scale
- Extreme wide: geography, isolation, scale, ritual, threat.
- Wide: body in environment, blocking, tactical movement.
- Medium: relationship, gesture, practical action.
- Medium close-up: emotion plus some body language.
- Close-up: decision, withheld emotion, pressure.
- Extreme close-up: evidence, tactile detail, anxiety, revelation.

## Angles
- Eye level: intimacy, realism, moral parity.
- Low angle: power, awe, menace, aspiration.
- High angle: vulnerability, surveillance, judgment.
- Dutch angle: instability; use sparingly.
- Over-the-shoulder: relationship geometry and point of view.
- Profile: distance, stalemate, withheld intimacy.

## Composition
- Centered symmetry: control, ritual, institutional pressure.
- Rule of thirds: naturalism and readable subject hierarchy.
- Negative space: loneliness, anticipation, unseen threat.
- Layered foreground/midground/background: depth and story context.
- Frame within frame: entrapment, surveillance, memory, social pressure.

## Blocking
Block for power and change. Track who owns space, who crosses boundaries, who turns away, who touches the prop, and who ends closer or farther away.

## Screen direction
Preserve left-right motion and eye lines unless disorientation is a deliberate story beat. If crossing the axis, include a re-anchoring shot.
